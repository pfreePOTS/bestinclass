However, the P&L only shows **$1,044,424** in the "Consulting Services - Cost" line.

**There is a $753,801 gap.** This means one of two things is happening:
1. $750K of contractor costs are being incorrectly coded to "Salary Expense" or another SG&A line item in QuickBooks, which artificially suppresses your gross margin and inflates your overhead.
2. The Expense Tracker contains $750K in projected or modeled costs that were not actually incurred and paid out in 2025.

Assuming the Expense Tracker is accurate to what you actually paid these people, your true COGS is much higher than the P&L reports, and your true SG&A is much lower.

## The True Source of Profit Leakage

When we allocate every dollar correctly—moving all delivery contractors to COGS, moving Flexis to COGS, and separating true overhead from delivery—the $738,444 profit gap comes from exactly three places:

### 1. Consulting/Project Margin Deficit: ~$400,000
Your delivery contractors (Basim, Blue Pisces, etc.) cost you nearly $1.8M, but you only billed $1.8M in consulting revenue. You are running the consulting division at near-zero gross margin. BIC firms target 48–52% margin. You are essentially passing through subcontractor labor at cost without a markup, or eating massive scope creep on fixed-fee projects.

### 2. Software Resale Pricing Loss: $185,000
You spent $562,726 buying standalone software (Synnex, Ingram, AWS) but only billed clients $514,405 for it. You lost $48,321. If priced correctly at the BIC 25% margin target, you would have made $137,000 in profit. This is a pure pricing failure.

### 3. G&A Overhead & Tool Sprawl: ~$150,000
Even after removing delivery labor, your G&A overhead includes $129,248 in Insurance Expense and $199,944 in Computer & Internet expenses (which contains ~$91,000 in redundant SaaS tool sprawl).
